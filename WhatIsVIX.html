What is the VIX and How is it Calculated?

What the VIX Measures:

  • The VIX reflects the implied volatility of the S&P 500 index (SPX).
  • It's derived from the prices of SPX options with near-term expirations.
  • Higher VIX values = higher expected volatility.
  • Lower VIX values = lower expected volatility.

How the VIX is Calculated (Simplified Overview):

  1. Out-of-the-money (OTM) SPX options:
    • Both call and put options that are not currently profitable.
    • Selected from options that expire in approximately 23 to 37 days.
  2. Implied volatility:
    • Backed out from the current prices of the options.
  3. Weighted average of variances:
    • Square root of the weighted average of option-implied variances.
    • Converted to a 30-day figure of expected annualized volatility.

VIX Formula (Conceptual Form):

VIX = 100 × sqrt[(2/T) × Σ(ΔK / K² × e^{RT} × Q(K)) - (1/T) × ((F / K₀) - 1)²]
  • T: Time to expiration
  • K: Strike price
  • ΔK: Difference between strike prices
  • R: Risk-free interest rate
  • Q(K): Midquote price of each option
  • F: Forward index level
  • K₀: First strike below the forward index level

TL;DR Summary:

  • The VIX measures the market’s forecast of volatility in the S&P 500 over the next month.
  • It’s calculated from SPX options prices using a formula that estimates expected volatility.
  • A VIX of 20 implies a 20% expected annual change (up or down) in the S&P 500.
